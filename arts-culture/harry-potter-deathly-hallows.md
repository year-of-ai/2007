# Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows

**Category:** Arts & Culture
**Key figures:** J. K. Rowling (author); Arthur A. Levine (Scholastic editor); Barry Cunningham (original UK publisher, Bloomsbury)

## Summary

Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows, the seventh and final novel in J. K. Rowling's Harry Potter series, was published simultaneously in the United Kingdom by Bloomsbury and in the United States by Scholastic on July 21, 2007. Rowling completed the manuscript on January 11, 2007, at the Balmoral Hotel in Edinburgh, Scotland — room 552, since renamed the J.K. Rowling Suite. The U.S. print run alone was 12 million copies — the largest initial print run in Scholastic's history at the time — with the UK Bloomsbury run adding a further 2.5 million.

The novel concludes the series' central conflict: Harry Potter, Ron Weasley, and Hermione Granger abandon Hogwarts to hunt down and destroy the Horcruxes — seven objects containing fragments of the dark wizard Voldemort's soul. The plot introduces the "Deathly Hallows," three legendary objects (the Elder Wand, the Resurrection Stone, and the Invisibility Cloak) that together supposedly confer mastery over death. The climax reveals that Harry himself is an unintentional Horcrux and must sacrifice himself before Voldemort can be killed. The epilogue, set nineteen years later, depicts Harry and his friends as adults with children of their own. The novel is 759 pages in the U.S. edition (Scholastic) and 607 pages in the UK edition (Bloomsbury), making it the second-longest book in the series after *Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix* (766 U.S. pages).

## Publication and Launch

### Spoiler Security

Security around the manuscript was unprecedented in publishing history. Scholastic and Bloomsbury imposed strict contractual requirements on printers, distributors, and booksellers. Books were shipped in sealed pallets only days before the release date; retailers were barred from displaying or selling copies until precisely 00:01 a.m. on July 21, 2007, under threat of losing future allocations. Despite these precautions, photographs of book pages appeared on internet forums and BitTorrent networks approximately four days before the official release, when an unknown person photographed all 759 pages of a copy obtained early. A court injunction was sought in the U.S. to prevent further distribution, and most major media outlets voluntarily agreed not to report specific plot details.

The U.S. retail chain Barnes & Noble estimated that 500,000 customers had pre-ordered the book online to receive it by mail on the release date — the largest pre-order in its history. Amazon.com reported a record number of pre-orders as well.

### Release Events

Midnight release parties took place at approximately 14,000 bookstores and libraries worldwide. In the United States, major retailers organized costume contests, trivia competitions, and theatrical performances. The Natural History Museum in London hosted a special launch event; Edinburgh Castle in Scotland hosted a ceremony for the UK launch, where Rowling gave a public reading. Fans gathered outside the Edinburgh venue included J.K. Rowling's family and several reporters who had been sworn to secrecy after reading advance copies.

### Sales Records

The book sold 8.3 million copies in the United States in its first 24 hours — a record set at 00:01 on July 21, 2007 when books became available — and 2.65 million copies in the United Kingdom over the same period. These figures earned it a Guinness World Record for the fastest-selling fiction book in history. U.S. sales reached 11.5 million copies within the first ten days. Scholastic's marketing campaign included a traveling Knight Bus visiting 40 libraries across the United States. Worldwide, the book sold approximately 44 million copies in its first year.

## Critical Reception

Critical reception was broadly positive, though notable reviewers expressed divided opinions on the ending. *The New York Times* gave it a major review on its front page — rare for a children's book — calling it "satisfying in its completeness." *The Guardian*'s review described Rowling's handling of the series' deaths as "brave and emotionally honest." Stephen King, writing in *Entertainment Weekly*, praised the book's pacing and characters while expressing mild reservations about the epilogue, which he called "a little too sweet for my taste."

The American Library Association named it a Best Book for Young Adults. The series conclusion also prompted widespread cultural commentary on the "end of childhood" for the generation of readers who had grown up with the books. *Time* magazine ran a cover story on the cultural reckoning accompanying the series' end, interviewing readers who had first encountered *Philosopher's Stone* as children and were now in their late teens and twenties.

The epilogue — "Nineteen Years Later" — generated particular discussion. Set in 2017, it depicts the adult Harry, Ron, Hermione, and Ginny sending their own children to Hogwarts, and it reveals the name of Harry's youngest child (Albus Severus Potter) as a tribute to two Hogwarts headmasters. Critics were divided: some found the closure emotionally satisfying; others, including King, felt the explicit happiness of the concluding scene undercut the book's darker themes.

## Significance

The Deathly Hallows publication marked the culmination of a publishing phenomenon that had transformed children's literature over a decade. Beginning with the release of Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone in 1997, the series had by 2007 sold more than 325 million copies worldwide across the first six volumes, been translated into 65 languages, and generated multiple major studio film adaptations. The series had been credited with reversing a decline in children's reading in the UK and United States and with making reading aspirational across age groups.

### Publishing Industry Impact

The economic scale of the series finale illustrated the book industry's capacity for blockbuster releases: midnight launch events at bookstores and libraries worldwide attracted millions of fans; the logistics required new distribution partnerships; and spoiler embargoes generated unprecedented advance secrecy, with booksellers and journalists agreeing not to reveal plot details before the official release time. The series demonstrated that children's fiction could generate revenues comparable to adult bestsellers — a commercial realignment that shaped publishers' acquisition strategies and marketing budgets for the subsequent decade.

The Deathly Hallows' commercial success came at a moment when the broader publishing industry was beginning to grapple with digital disruption. The [Amazon Kindle](../science-technology/amazon-kindle-launch.md) launched just four months later, in November 2007. That the largest print run in Scholastic's history sold out in hours while Amazon was simultaneously preparing to launch a device designed to replace print demonstrated the two competing trajectories of reading culture in 2007.

### Film Adaptations

Two film adaptations of the Deathly Hallows — Part 1 (released November 19, 2010) and Part 2 (released July 15, 2011) — were produced by Warner Bros., with screenwriter Steve Kloves adapting the novel and David Yates directing both films. Part 2 grossed $1.342 billion worldwide to become the twelfth-highest-grossing film of all time at that point, and it won the series its highest-profile film accolades, including nominations for three Academy Awards (Visual Effects, Makeup, Art Direction). The decision to split the final book into two films — announced by Warner Bros. in June 2007, weeks before the book's release — established a commercial template followed by *Twilight: Breaking Dawn* (2011–2012) and *The Hunger Games: Mockingjay* (2014–2015).

### YA Publishing Category

The Harry Potter franchise catalyzed the broader "young adult" publishing category into a commercial force capable of competing with adult mainstream fiction, directly influencing the rise of subsequent franchise series such as Twilight (Stephenie Meyer, 2005–2008) and The Hunger Games (Suzanne Collins, 2008–2010). Publishers recognized that children and teenagers would read long, complex books without compromise — a counterintuitive lesson that reshaped how the industry positioned and marketed literature for younger readers throughout the 2010s.
