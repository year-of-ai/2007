# YouTube's Rise as Global Media Platform in 2007

**Category:** Science & Technology
**Key figures:** Steve Chen (YouTube co-founder), Chad Hurley (YouTube CEO), Jawed Karim (YouTube co-founder), Eric Schmidt (Google CEO), Steve Grove (YouTube Head of News and Politics), David Bohrman (CNN Washington Bureau Chief), Anderson Cooper (CNN debate moderator)

## Summary

By 2007, YouTube had transformed from a 2005 startup novelty into a defining piece of global media infrastructure. Following Google's $1.65 billion acquisition in November 2006, YouTube in 2007 achieved unprecedented scale: over 100 million videos watched daily, with users uploading approximately 65,000 new videos per day—equivalent to roughly 6 hours of content added every minute by mid-year, rising to 10 hours per minute by year's end. The platform's monthly active user base grew from approximately 20 million in early 2006 to an estimated 160 million by late 2007—a roughly 700% increase. YouTube's 2007 milestone was not merely technical growth but structural transformation: the company launched its Partner Program enabling creators to share advertising revenue, forged an integration with Apple's iPhone that reached a new mobile audience, hosted the first citizen-question U.S. presidential debates in partnership with CNN, and localized operations across multiple countries. By year's end, YouTube had established itself simultaneously as a media platform, a creator-economy engine, a tool for democratic participation, and a cultural institution.

## Background: From Startup to Google Property

YouTube was founded in February 2005 by three former PayPal employees—Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim—in a garage in Menlo Park, California. The site launched publicly in December 2005 and was acquired by Google on October 9, 2006, for $1.65 billion in stock. At acquisition, YouTube served approximately 100 million video views per day and had 65,000 daily uploads—remarkable figures that nonetheless represented a fraction of what the platform would achieve twelve months later. The acquisition gave YouTube Google's infrastructure, advertising technology (including the DoubleClick ad-serving platform that Google separately acquired in 2007 for $3.1 billion), and global operational scale. Chad Hurley remained as CEO through 2010, with Salar Kamangar subsequently becoming YouTube's first dedicated CEO as it operated as an independent subsidiary.

## Growth Metrics in 2007

### Scale of Consumption and Upload

By mid-2007, YouTube served more than 100 million video views daily. The upload rate climbed from roughly 65,000 videos per day at the time of Google's acquisition (October 2006) to a pace equivalent to about 6 hours of video uploaded every minute by mid-2007, accelerating to roughly 10 hours every minute by late 2007. This rate—10 hours per minute—meant that in a single hour, more content was uploaded to YouTube than the three major U.S. television networks (ABC, CBS, NBC) had broadcast in their combined history. The platform's bandwidth requirements were themselves a milestone: by 2007, YouTube consumed as much internet bandwidth as the entire global internet had in the year 2000.

### User Base Growth

Monthly active users grew from an estimated 20 million in early 2006 to 160 million by late 2007. Unique visitor data from comScore, the leading web analytics provider at the time, confirmed YouTube's dominant position in online video: in January 2007, YouTube accounted for approximately 60% of all videos watched online in the United States. The platform was receiving over 50 million unique monthly visitors in the United States alone by mid-year, with international users accounting for a growing share.

## Key Milestones of 2007

### YouTube Partner Program Launch

In May 2007, YouTube launched its Partner Program, enabling content creators who met specified subscriber and view thresholds to monetize their videos through in-stream advertising. Under the program, YouTube shared 55% of advertising revenue with creators—a ratio that became the industry standard and that YouTube has maintained to the present. This was a structurally transformative moment: it created the economic foundation for a professional creator class that was not employed by or dependent on traditional media institutions.

The first YouTube Partners included established online video pioneers. By the end of 2007, several dozen creators had been accepted into the program. The policy established the precedent that individual video creators could earn a sustainable income from their content—the economic model of what would later be called the "creator economy" was formalized in 2007, even though that term did not yet exist. The first Partner to earn $1 million from YouTube would do so by 2008.

### CNN/YouTube Presidential Debates

On July 23, 2007, CNN hosted the Democratic primary presidential debate using citizen-submitted YouTube video questions—the first time a major U.S. presidential debate incorporated user-generated internet content as its primary questioning mechanism. CNN anchor Anderson Cooper moderated. Approximately 3,000 video questions were submitted by American voters; CNN selected and showed approximately 39 during the broadcast. Questions came from diverse individuals including a snowman asking about climate change, a gay military officer asking about "Don't Ask, Don't Tell," and ordinary voters in diners and living rooms asking about healthcare and the economy.

The format was initially controversial among political analysts who questioned whether internet-mediated question selection would trivialize the debate. The results demonstrated the opposite: the citizen questions were frequently more direct and personally relevant than those from professional journalists, and the debate drew 2.6 million viewers—higher than comparable primary debates without citizen questions. CNN hosted a Republican primary debate in the same format on November 28, 2007, moderated by Anderson Cooper, with Mitt Romney, Rudy Giuliani, Mike Huckabee, John McCain, and other candidates. These debates established a precedent for citizen-question formats that influenced subsequent election cycles.

### iPhone Integration (June 2007)

Apple's iPhone, launched on June 29, 2007, shipped with a YouTube app as one of its small number of native applications—a dedicated icon on the original iPhone's home screen alongside Mail, Phone, iPod, and Safari. This integration was the product of a direct partnership between Google and Apple negotiated as part of the iPhone launch. The YouTube iPhone app encoded videos in H.264 format (the mobile-optimized successor to Flash-based web video) rather than the Adobe Flash that YouTube used for web delivery, which iPhones could not run.

The partnership had two lasting effects. First, it gave YouTube immediate access to the entire iPhone user base—estimated at 1.4 million units sold in the first quarter after launch—without requiring users to navigate to a website. Second, it forced YouTube to begin transcoding its video library from Flash Video to H.264, an expensive infrastructure investment that proved prescient: the subsequent decline of Adobe Flash and rise of HTML5 video made H.264 the universal web video format within five years. YouTube's early H.264 investment, driven by iPhone compatibility, positioned it ahead of competitors for the mobile video era.

### Verizon MMS Partnership (July 2007)

YouTube announced a partnership with Verizon Wireless in July 2007 enabling Verizon subscribers to upload videos from their mobile phones to YouTube via MMS (Multimedia Messaging Service). This predated the smartphone era's dominance—the majority of Verizon's customer base in 2007 carried feature phones, not smartphones—and demonstrated YouTube's effort to extend mobile access beyond the early iPhone audience. The partnership made YouTube one of the first web platforms to build a mobile upload path specifically for feature phone users, recognizing that mobile video capture and sharing would grow faster than smartphone adoption.

### International Localization

YouTube launched localized versions in multiple markets in 2007, including Brazil, France, Italy, Japan, the Netherlands, Poland, Spain, and the United Kingdom. This expansion added native-language interfaces, local trending content, and country-specific advertising. The localization strategy recognized that English-language domination of the platform's early years was not sustainable as the platform scaled; by year's end, international users constituted a majority of YouTube's traffic. The localization also provided a foundation for YouTube to become a tool for political organizing and documentation in non-English-speaking countries, a function that would become prominent in subsequent years.

### Viral Culture and the Lonelygirl15 Phenomenon

The web series *Lonelygirl15* had launched in June 2006 as an apparently authentic video diary by a teenager named "Bree" with strict religious parents. In September 2006, investigative viewers and journalists revealed that "Bree" was Jessica Rose, a New Zealand–raised American actress, and the series was a scripted production. Rather than killing the series, the revelation intensified public interest in 2007: by early 2007, the channel had over 100,000 subscribers and millions of views, making Jessica Rose one of the first individuals to achieve celebrity status primarily through YouTube. She won the Webby Award for Best Actress in 2007.

The YouTube Awards, established in March 2007, gave formal recognition to user-generated content creators across categories including Most Creative, Most Adorable, Most Inspiring, and Best Comedy. These awards institutionalized the concept of internet stardom—distinct from Hollywood or music industry celebrity, born from direct creator-to-viewer relationships at scale.

## Technical Infrastructure

YouTube's 2007 scale created substantial engineering challenges. The platform's video delivery required investment in content delivery networks (CDNs) distributed globally to reduce latency, and Google's infrastructure acquisition played a central role in enabling this. YouTube in 2007 was estimated to be consuming approximately 1% of all internet bandwidth—a figure that concentrated media attention on the technical implications of user-generated video at scale.

A key format transition began in 2007: the platform began encoding an increasing portion of its library in H.264 (MPEG-4 Part 10) alongside the existing Flash Video format. Flash Video had been the standard for web video since the mid-2000s, but it was not supported on Apple's iOS platform (a position Apple maintained intentionally). The H.264 transition, accelerated by the iPhone partnership, began what would become a multi-year migration from Flash-dependent web video to universally accessible HTML5 video—a transition that Adobe formally acknowledged by ending Flash for mobile in 2011 and for desktop in 2020.

## Significance

### Democratization of Video Production

Prior to YouTube's scale, video production required either expensive equipment and technical expertise or access to institutional gatekeepers (broadcast networks, cable channels, or film studios). YouTube's ease of use and near-zero distribution cost meant that individuals with a consumer video camera, or eventually a mobile phone, could reach an audience of millions. The 2007 milestones solidified this shift: the Partner Program provided an economic model, the CNN debates provided a civic legitimacy, and the iPhone integration provided a hardware pathway to broader mobile participation.

### Creator Economy Foundation

The Partner Program's 55% revenue-sharing model established the economic architecture of the creator economy years before that term entered common use. By enabling individual creators to earn income from advertising revenue generated by their audiences, YouTube created a new labor market—independent content creation—that would grow from dozens of partners in 2007 to more than 2 million creators in the YouTube Partner Program by 2020 and over 50 million self-described "creators" across platforms by the mid-2020s.

### Democratic Participation and Civic Media

The CNN/YouTube debates demonstrated that internet platforms could serve as civic infrastructure, not merely entertainment. The debates' citizen-question format proved that digital tools could enhance democratic participation and citizen voice in the political process, even at the scale of a major U.S. presidential primary. The format also established that major broadcast outlets (CNN) would partner with digital platforms rather than compete with them—a precedent for the subsequent decade's media convergence.

### Mobile and Infrastructure Investment

YouTube's iPhone partnership and Verizon MMS integration established early 2007 as the moment when web video began its transition to mobile-first delivery. The H.264 encoding investment YouTube made to support iPhone compatibility proved foundational to the subsequent decade's streaming landscape. The CDN infrastructure built out to support YouTube's 2007 traffic levels contributed to the broader buildout of internet capacity that enabled Netflix's streaming expansion (which began in 2007 with the Watch Instantly feature) and the subsequent streaming-first entertainment landscape.

### Platform Governance Challenges

YouTube's rapid 2007 growth also surfaced governance challenges that would persist. In March 2007, Viacom filed a $1 billion copyright infringement lawsuit against YouTube, alleging that 160,000 unauthorized clips of Viacom's content—including Paramount, MTV, and Comedy Central material—had been viewed more than 1.5 billion times on the platform. This lawsuit, combined with similar claims from other rights holders, initiated the decade-long debate over safe harbor protections under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) Section 512 and the appropriate responsibilities of platforms for user-uploaded content. YouTube's 2007 introduction of Content ID—an automated content recognition system that matched uploaded videos against a database of copyrighted works and allowed rights holders to monetize or remove matching content—was the platform's primary technical response. Content ID represented an early example of algorithmic content governance at scale.
