# Luciano Pavarotti

**Category:** People
**Key figures:** Luciano Pavarotti (tenor); Placido Domingo (tenor, Three Tenors partner); José Carreras (tenor, Three Tenors partner); Herbert von Karajan (early conductor/mentor); James Levine (Met Opera conductor, frequent collaborator)

## Summary

Luciano Pavarotti (October 12, 1935 – September 6, 2007), widely considered the greatest operatic tenor of the second half of the 20th century, died of pancreatic cancer at his home in Modena, Italy, at age 71. His 46-year career—from his professional operatic debut on April 29, 1961, in Reggio Emilia to his farewell performance at the closing ceremony of the 2006 Winter Olympics in Turin—transformed opera from a specialist art form into mass popular entertainment and generated a commercial and cultural legacy without parallel in classical music.

Pavarotti made his debut at the Metropolitan Opera in New York on November 23, 1968, as Rodolfo in *La Bohème*, the role that would become his signature. Over the next 35 years he gave more than 380 performances at the Met alone. His voice was celebrated above all for its upper register: his ability to sustain clear, powerful high Cs with apparent effortlessness earned him the popular title "the King of the High Cs," most famously demonstrated in a 1972 Metropolitan Opera broadcast of Donizetti's *La fille du régiment*, in which he sang nine consecutive high Cs without strain.

## Career

### Early Life and Training

Born in Modena in the Emilia-Romagna region of Italy, Pavarotti grew up in a household where his father, Fernando, sang as an amateur tenor in a local choir. He initially trained as a teacher and worked briefly as a schoolteacher before committing to vocal training under Arrigo Pola, a professional tenor, and later Ettore Campogalliani. His professional debut—as Rodolfo in Puccini's *La Bohème* in Reggio Emilia on April 29, 1961—was followed rapidly by engagements across Italy and then internationally.

His international breakthrough came when soprano Joan Sutherland invited him to tour Australia with her company in 1965. His performances there attracted international attention and established his reputation. By 1966 he was performing regularly at the Royal Opera House Covent Garden in London; his La Scala debut followed in 1967.

### Metropolitan Opera Career

Pavarotti's relationship with the Metropolitan Opera in New York was the central institutional partnership of his career. From his 1968 debut through his final Met appearances in 2004, he gave 380 performances across 17 roles. James Levine, who conducted many of those performances, later recalled that Pavarotti's voice was unique in combining "warmth, squillo [ring], and technical command in a single instrument." His most performed roles at the Met were Rodolfo (*La Bohème*), Riccardo (*Un ballo in maschera*), the Duke (*Rigoletto*), Cavaradossi (*Tosca*), and Radamès (*Aida*).

### Three Tenors

The event that brought Pavarotti to a global audience beyond opera was the Three Tenors concert, performed on July 7, 1990, at the ancient Baths of Caracalla in Rome the night before the 1990 FIFA World Cup final. Pavarotti, Placido Domingo, and José Carreras—three of the world's leading tenors—performed under conductor Zubin Mehta before a live audience of approximately 6,000 and a television audience estimated at 800 million viewers across 42 countries. The concert album sold over 10 million copies, making it the best-selling classical music album in history at the time and introducing operatic repertoire to a mass audience that had never attended an opera.

The Three Tenors subsequently performed at World Cup concerts in Los Angeles (1994), Paris (1998), and Yokohama (2002), as well as dozens of stadium concerts worldwide throughout the 1990s. The commercial model—open-air stadium performances of operatic highlights marketed as popular events—was new to classical music and proved enormously lucrative, drawing criticism from traditionalists who felt it cheapened opera while simultaneously generating hundreds of millions of new listeners.

### Final Years and Death

Pavarotti's final performance was at the opening ceremony of the 2006 Winter Olympics in Turin, Italy, on February 10, 2006, where he sang "Nessun dorma" from Puccini's *Turandot*—the aria that had become his most recognizable signature. The performance was later recognized as his public farewell; he was visibly ill and required assistance walking to the stage. In August 2006 he was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer. He died on September 6, 2007, in Modena. Italian President Giorgio Napolitano ordered national flags flown at half-mast; his funeral on September 8 in Modena Cathedral drew dignitaries, musicians, and thousands of mourners, and was broadcast live internationally.

## Significance

Pavarotti's death prompted tributes from world leaders, opera companies, and millions of fans worldwide, reflecting his status as perhaps the most recognized classical musician since Leonard Bernstein. His significance operates on several levels:

- **Artistic standard:** His voice set the benchmark for lyric and spinto tenors in the 20th century. His recording of Puccini and Donizetti arias remains the definitive commercial reference for those repertories. He won three Grammy Awards: in 1980 for *O Sole Mio* (Traditional Pop Vocal Performance) and in 1981 and 1987 for classical vocal albums.

- **Democratization of opera:** The Three Tenors model proved that opera could command audiences in the tens of millions if presented accessibly. This shifted opera marketing globally, inspiring similar crossover formats (Andrea Bocelli, Il Divo) that have brought classical and operatic music to audiences who would not attend a traditional opera house.

- **Commercial impact:** Pavarotti's recordings sold over 100 million copies worldwide across his career. His late-career *Pavarotti & Friends* charity concerts at the Parco Novi Sad in Modena (1992–2000), which paired him with rock and pop artists including Sting, U2, Celine Dion, Mariah Carey, and Elton John, raised approximately $18 million for humanitarian causes including UNICEF and War Child.

- **Cultural ambassador:** At a time when Italy's cultural visibility in the English-speaking world was primarily through film and fashion, Pavarotti served as a global ambassador for Italian musical culture on a scale unmatched since Enrico Caruso in the early 20th century.
