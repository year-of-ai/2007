# Mahmoud Ahmadinejad's Columbia University speech

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Mahmoud Ahmadinejad (Iranian president), Lee Bollinger (Columbia University president), John Coatsworth (Dean, School of International and Public Affairs), George W. Bush, Iranian Foreign Ministry

## Summary

On September 24, 2007, Iranian President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad delivered a controversial speech and took questions at Columbia University's World Leaders Forum in New York City. The event occurred during Ahmadinejad's visit to the United States to address the United Nations General Assembly, making it a focal point for tensions between the United States and Iran during the final years of the Bush administration. Despite intense pressure from political leaders, Jewish organizations, and human rights activists urging Columbia to cancel the invitation, the university proceeded with the forum.

Columbia University President Lee Bollinger delivered a publicly confrontational introduction of unusual severity, describing Ahmadinejad as exhibiting "all the signs of a petty and cruel dictator" and criticizing his statements on the Holocaust as "simply ridiculous." Ahmadinejad responded by characterizing the introduction as "an insult to information and knowledge of the audience there" and likened the atmosphere to a student being scolded by a professor. During his subsequent 90-minute remarks, Ahmadinejad addressed U.S. foreign policy in the Middle East, Iran's nuclear program (which he maintained was entirely peaceful), and responded to pointed questions from students and faculty.

## Background: Invitation and Controversy

Columbia University's World Leaders Forum, established in 2003, regularly invited heads of state and other prominent figures to engage with the academic community, on the stated principle that direct engagement — however uncomfortable — advanced understanding. The university's dealings with Ahmadinejad specifically had a fraught precedent: an invitation for him to speak at the School of International and Public Affairs in 2006 had been withdrawn amid logistical concerns and President Bollinger's stated doubts about whether the appearance would reflect the forum's academic values.

The 2007 invitation, arranged through faculty back-channels and backed by Bollinger, revived that earlier, aborted plan. Bollinger later argued that withdrawing the invitation a second time after public opposition emerged would have made Columbia appear to capitulate to political pressure — a violation of the university's commitment to open discourse.

Opposition to the event was immediate and sustained:

- **New York Governor Eliot Spitzer** called the invitation "deeply offensive."
- **New York City Mayor Michael Bloomberg**, though a strong free-speech advocate, described Ahmadinejad as a "reckless" leader.
- The **New York State Assembly** passed a resolution calling on Columbia to rescind the invitation.
- **Jewish organizations** including the Anti-Defamation League and American Jewish Congress organized protests outside Columbia's Morningside Heights campus on the day of the speech.
- Several **Columbia alumni** and **donors** threatened to withhold contributions in response to the invitation.
- **U.S. Senate** and **House** members from both parties criticized the decision, with some calling it an inappropriate platform for a state sponsor of terrorism.

The day before the speech, Ahmadinejad's request to lay a wreath at the Ground Zero site was denied by New York City authorities.

## The Speech

Ahmadinejad arrived at Roone Arledge Auditorium at Columbia's Lerner Hall on September 24 to a hall filled with approximately 700 students, faculty, and invited guests, as well as a large international press contingent. Several hundred protestors gathered outside.

**Bollinger's Introduction**: Bollinger's introduction was itself newsworthy for its bluntness. He stated that Ahmadinejad had exhibited "all the signs of a petty and cruel dictator," called him "either brazenly provocative or astonishingly uneducated" for his Holocaust statements, and directly challenged him on Iran's support for groups designated as terrorist organizations by the U.S. government, Iran's provision of weapons to forces attacking U.S. troops in Iraq, and the arrest and imprisonment of Iranian-American scholars. Bollinger framed the event explicitly as "not a debate — it is an encounter with a man who leads a government that has many similarities with the Nazis."

**Ahmadinejad's Response**: Ahmadinejad began by objecting to Bollinger's introduction, saying: "In Iran, tradition requires that when we invite a person to be a speaker, we respect them." He delivered remarks through a translator covering several themes:

- **Nuclear program**: Ahmadinejad reiterated that Iran's uranium enrichment was for peaceful civilian energy purposes, not weapons development, and accused the U.S. and Western powers of hypocrisy given their own nuclear arsenals.
- **Holocaust**: He called for "more research" on the Holocaust, a position widely characterized as Holocaust denial, and suggested that Europeans should bear responsibility for Israel's existence rather than Palestinians.
- **Homosexuality in Iran**: When asked directly whether Iran persecuted gay people, Ahmadinejad replied: "In Iran, we don't have homosexuals like in your country." The statement — met with audible laughter and jeers from the audience — was particularly striking given that Iran had been documented executing men for homosexual conduct. Human rights organizations estimated that Iran had executed over 4,000 gay men since the 1979 Islamic Revolution.
- **Palestine and Israel**: He called for a referendum of all inhabitants of historical Palestine — including Jews, Christians, and Muslims — to determine the region's political future, a formulation that would effectively dissolve Israel as a Jewish state.
- **Iraq**: He accused the United States of destabilizing the region and denied that Iran was supplying weapons to armed groups in Iraq, contradicting public assessments by U.S. military officials.

The question-and-answer session that followed was moderated by Coatsworth. Students pressed Ahmadinejad on the execution of minors in Iran, the status of women and dissidents, and the nuclear program. The exchange was widely broadcast in real time.

## Reactions

**In the United States**: The response divided broadly along free-speech and political lines. Defenders of Columbia's decision — including many within the academic community and civil liberties organizations — argued that engaging with adversarial world leaders was exactly what universities existed to do, and that Bollinger's confrontational introduction demonstrated intellectual courage rather than deference. Critics countered that any platform granted to Ahmadinejad, however adversarial its framing, lent him undeserved legitimacy and provided propaganda value to the Iranian government. The White House stated that Ahmadinejad's visit was "an insult" to the victims of September 11 and to the U.S. troops he claimed Iran was not supplying in Iraq.

**In Iran**: Iranian state media, which had full discretion over how to frame the event domestically, largely omitted Bollinger's confrontational introduction from its coverage and presented the speech as Ahmadinejad successfully challenging American aggression before an elite audience. The Iranian Foreign Ministry called Bollinger's introduction "rude and discourteous." Hardline Iranian newspapers celebrated the event as a propaganda victory; reformist Iranian media noted that being challenged on his record before a live audience was something Ahmadinejad never faced at home.

**Congressional response**: Several members of Congress called for a review of the State Department's decision to grant Ahmadinejad a visa for U.N. General Assembly attendance and criticized the Columbia visit. Representative Duncan Hunter introduced legislation to defund Columbia from federal research grants, though it did not advance.

## Context: U.S.–Iran Relations in 2007

The Columbia speech occurred at a moment of acute Iranian-American tension. The Bush administration had, in the preceding months, significantly escalated pressure on Iran:

- In January 2007, U.S. forces raided an Iranian liaison office in Erbil, Iraq, arresting five Iranian officials whom the administration accused of facilitating attacks on U.S. troops — a significant escalation in direct U.S.–Iran confrontation.
- In August 2007, the Bush administration designated Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) a terrorist organization — the first time a state's military branch had been so designated.
- Simultaneously, the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) continued to dispute Iran's nuclear program disclosures, and the UN Security Council had imposed two rounds of sanctions on Iran in December 2006 and March 2007.

The speech thus occurred within a pattern of escalating confrontation that shaped the reception of Ahmadinejad's every word. His appearance at Columbia was inseparable from debates then raging about the [Iraq War troop surge](iraq-war-troop-surge.md) and its relationship to Iranian influence in Iraq, and from broader Western concerns about authoritarian state power in a moment when [Gordon Brown had succeeded Tony Blair](gordon-brown-becomes-pm.md) as Britain's prime minister, altering the transatlantic coalition against Iran's nuclear program.

## Significance

The Columbia speech became a landmark in the debate over how democratic societies — and their institutions — should engage with authoritarian leaders. It embodied the tension between two legitimate principles: the academic commitment to open inquiry and direct engagement on the one hand, and the moral cost of providing a platform to a leader whose government denied the Holocaust, executed gay men, and repressed political dissent on the other.

The event also illustrated the gap between intended academic dialogue and political reality. Bollinger's calculated confrontation largely neutralized the propaganda value Ahmadinejad might have extracted from a respectful platform — but it also raised the question of whether a confrontational "debate" format served the cause of understanding any better than refusal. In Iran, the event was presented as Ahmadinejad successfully representing Iranian views before a hostile foreign audience, demonstrating that the same event could carry opposite meanings in different information environments.

The speech remained a reference point in subsequent debates over campus free speech, the hosting of controversial figures by universities, and the broader question of how Western institutions should engage with illiberal regimes.
