# Darfur Conflict in 2007

**Category:** History & Politics
**Key figures:** Omar al-Bashir (Sudan President), Ahmed Haroun (State Minister of Humanitarian Affairs), Ali Kushayb (Janjaweed militia commander), Khalil Ibrahim (JEM rebel leader), Abdul Wahid al Nur (SLA commander), Bill Richardson (U.S. mediator), Rodolphe Adada (AU–UN Joint Special Representative)

## Summary

The conflict in Darfur, a region of western Sudan, emerged in February 2003 and had become, by 2007, what the United Nations and international humanitarian organizations recognized as "the world's worst humanitarian crisis." In 2007, the crisis reached a turning point marked by intensified international intervention and the first concrete steps toward international accountability. The conflict pitted the Arab-led Sudanese government and allied Janjaweed militia against various rebel groups—primarily the Sudan Liberation Army (SLA) and the Justice and Equality Movement (JEM)—representing the Fur, Masalit, and Zaghawa ethnic minorities. By the end of 2007, the estimated death toll ranged from 200,000 to 450,000, with over 2.7 million people displaced from their homes across Darfur's three federal states: North Darfur, South Darfur, and West Darfur.

## Background

Darfur's conflict had roots in longstanding ethnic and economic competition between Arab nomadic herders and non-Arab sedentary farming communities intensified by desertification and land pressure from the expanding Sahara. When the SLA and JEM launched armed attacks on government targets in February 2003—citing political and economic marginalization of Darfur's non-Arab population—the Sudanese government responded with aerial bombardment of villages and facilitation of Janjaweed militia raids targeting civilian populations. From 2003 to 2007, this counterinsurgency strategy produced mass atrocities: village burning, systematic rape, looting of food stores, and displacement of millions from agricultural lands that sustained livelihoods.

The African Union Mission in Sudan (AMIS), deployed from 2004, lacked the personnel, equipment, mandate, and financial resources to protect civilians or deter violence. By 2007, AMIS's approximately 7,000 troops—authorized but often not fully deployed or equipped—were widely regarded as unable to address the scale and severity of the crisis.

## Key Events of 2007

### ICC Arrest Warrants Issued (February 2007)

On February 27, 2007, the International Criminal Court issued its first arrest warrants related to Darfur, formally charging Ahmed Haroun—Sudan's State Minister of Humanitarian Affairs—and Ali Kushayb—a senior Janjaweed militia commander—with 51 counts of crimes against humanity and war crimes. The charges included murder, rape, torture, pillaging, and the forcible transfer of population at specific named locations including Kodoom, Bindisi, Mukjar, Deleig, Garsila, and Arawala, and covering the period from August 2003 to March 2004.

These were the first ICC arrest warrants issued against an individual still serving in a government ministry—Haroun was actively overseeing the Darfur Humanitarian Affairs Commission at the time—and signaled that international accountability mechanisms could be brought to bear against sitting officials. Sudan refused to surrender either individual, and both remained at liberty, but the warrants established a legal record of the ICC's jurisdiction over the case and created long-term reputational and travel consequences for both men.

### U.S. Genocide Designation Intensified (May 2007)

The United States had first formally designated the Darfur violence as genocide on September 9, 2004—making it the first time any government had applied the term to an ongoing conflict under the UN Genocide Convention framework. In May 2007, the U.S. State Department reaffirmed and intensified this designation, tightening economic sanctions on Sudanese entities. The Bush administration announced targeted sanctions against 30 companies in Sudan's oil, defense, and shipping sectors, as well as measures against three Sudanese individuals deemed responsible for violence or obstruction of peace processes. The oil sector restrictions were particularly significant: China's deep economic ties to Sudan's oil industry (China was Sudan's largest oil customer, importing approximately 65% of Sudan's oil exports) meant U.S. pressure on Sudan required navigating Sino-American diplomatic complexity.

### Sudan-Chad Dakar Peace Accord (May 2007)

Sudan and Chad had accused each other of supporting rebel groups since 2005—Sudan backing Chadian rebel movements and Chad sheltering Darfuri rebel groups including the JEM. This proxy conflict threatened regional stability across the broader Sahel. On May 3, 2007, in Riyadh, Sudan and Chad signed a peace accord brokered with mediation from Saudi Arabia and Qatar. A subsequent meeting in Dakar on March 13, 2008 (often grouped with the May 2007 process as the "Dakar Accord") established a contact group and normalized diplomatic relations. U.S. Governor Bill Richardson, serving as special envoy, worked alongside African Union mediators in multiple rounds of negotiations aimed at preventing full-scale interstate war between Sudan and Chad.

### UN Security Council Resolution 1769 and UNAMID (July–December 2007)

The most significant international institutional development of 2007 was UN Security Council Resolution 1769, passed on July 31, 2007. The resolution authorized the African Union–United Nations Hybrid Operation in Darfur (UNAMID) with a maximum authorized strength of 26,000 military personnel and police—at that point the largest peacekeeping force ever authorized by the UN Security Council. UNAMID was structured as a "hybrid" force at Sudan's insistence; its African character was intended to address Khartoum's objections to a purely UN-led force, which the Sudanese government characterized as neo-colonial intervention. China and Russia, both permanent Security Council members, had resisted earlier draft resolutions calling for a UN-only force with a Chapter VII enforcement mandate; Resolution 1769's hybrid compromise reflected that geopolitical reality.

The formal handover from AMIS to UNAMID was completed on December 31, 2007. Rodolphe Adada of the Republic of the Congo was appointed as the Joint AU-UN Special Representative and head of mission. However, UNAMID faced immediate challenges: Sudan restricted helicopter operations, delayed issuance of entry visas for troops and equipment, and denied overflight clearances, severely hampering the mission's ability to deploy quickly or operate effectively across Darfur's approximately 493,000 square kilometers. By December 31, 2007, only a fraction of the authorized force was actually deployed.

### Failed Peace Negotiations at Sirte (October 2007)

International mediators convened the Sirte II talks in Tripoli, Libya, in October 2007, hoping to build on the partially successful 2006 Abuja Peace Agreement (which SLA-Minawi commander Minni Minawi had signed but major SLA factions and the JEM had rejected). The October 2007 Sirte negotiations failed to achieve a comprehensive ceasefire. Abdul Wahid al-Nur's SLA faction boycotted the talks; the JEM attended but refused to sign any agreement without first securing a meeting with President al-Bashir. The Sudanese government delegation agreed in principle to some humanitarian access provisions but resisted accountability language. The fractured rebel landscape—with more than a dozen SLA splinter groups by late 2007—made unified representation at peace talks nearly impossible, giving the government an incentive to delay rather than engage substantively.

## Humanitarian Conditions

The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) documented that approximately 4.2 million people in Darfur were dependent on humanitarian aid by late 2007—a figure representing the majority of the region's estimated 5.5–6 million total population. Roughly 2.7 million of these were internally displaced persons (IDPs), living in camps or informal settlements across Darfur or in neighboring regions. An additional 250,000+ Darfuri refugees had fled across the border into Chad, residing in 12 UNHCR-managed camps in eastern Chad.

Mortality surveys conducted in 2007 by the World Health Organization and international NGOs estimated a crude mortality rate still exceeding emergency thresholds in several camp settings. Malnutrition, diarrheal diseases, and respiratory infections were the leading causes of excess death. Access for humanitarian organizations—including the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC), Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF), and UN World Food Programme (WFP)—remained severely restricted by insecurity, bureaucratic obstruction, and direct attacks on aid workers. In 2007, at least 12 humanitarian workers were killed in Darfur and approximately 150 aid vehicles were carjacked—a significant deterioration from prior years that threatened the ability of aid organizations to maintain operations.

## International Political Dynamics

### China's Role

China's position was central to understanding the constraints on international action. China had built a $4 billion oil industry infrastructure in Sudan through the China National Petroleum Corporation (CNPC) and was importing approximately 65% of Sudan's oil production. China held Sudan's third-largest bilateral aid and investment relationship in Africa. Beijing blocked strong UN Security Council resolutions throughout 2004–2007, and its abstention (rather than veto) on Resolution 1769 was the product of sustained diplomatic pressure, including public activism directed at the 2008 Beijing Olympics. "Genocide Olympics" campaigns in the United States and Europe in 2007 created pressure on China's image ahead of the 2008 Games, which contributed to Beijing's eventual support for the hybrid mission compromise.

### The "Responsibility to Protect" Debate

Darfur in 2007 became a central reference case in debates over the Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine, adopted unanimously at the 2005 UN World Summit. R2P established that the international community had a responsibility to intervene when a state failed to protect its own citizens from mass atrocities. Darfur tested whether R2P could translate from unanimous declaration to actual intervention. The constraints revealed—Security Council vetoes, national sovereignty arguments, and operational capacity limits—exposed a significant gap between the doctrine's aspirations and its practical implementation.

## Significance

The 2007 turning point in Darfur marked a shift from international expressions of concern to concrete, if constrained, intervention. The ICC prosecutions signaled that international criminal law could reach sitting government officials—a precedent that would directly shape subsequent ICC cases against al-Bashir himself (indicted in 2009 and 2010) and inform accountability mechanisms in subsequent conflicts. UNAMID's establishment, however compromised by Sudan's restrictions, was the largest peacekeeping authorization in UN history at the time of passage, reflecting the growing international consensus that mass atrocity prevention required robust multilateral commitment.

Geopolitically, Darfur exemplified the post-Cold War tension between humanitarian intervention and national sovereignty, complicated by the energy politics that made Sudan relevant to Chinese and Western strategic interests. The crisis also demonstrated that international media attention—sustained across nearly four years by consistent reporting—could maintain political pressure on governments to act but could not by itself resolve a conflict where the primary perpetrator held state military power.

The Darfur conflict coincided with several other major international events of 2007. The same year, the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report and the Nobel Peace Prize awarded jointly to Al Gore and the IPCC elevated global attention to climate change and resource scarcity—dynamics that had contributed to the desertification pressures underlying Darfur's origins. The global oil price spike of 2007 also heightened stakes around Sudan's oil production, tying Darfur's humanitarian crisis to international energy markets in ways that complicated diplomatic responses.
