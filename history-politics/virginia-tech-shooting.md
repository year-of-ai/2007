# Virginia Tech Shooting

**Category:** History & Politics
**Key figures:** Seung-Hui Cho (perpetrator); Liviu Librescu (faculty victim); Governor Tim Kaine (Virginia); President George W. Bush

## Summary

On April 16, 2007, Seung-Hui Cho, a 23-year-old senior English major and South Korean national with U.S. permanent resident status, carried out two sequential attacks on the campus of Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University (Virginia Tech) in Blacksburg, Virginia. The assault began at approximately 7:15 a.m. when Cho shot two students — freshman Emily Hilscher and resident assistant Ryan Clark — in West Ambler Johnston Hall, a dormitory. University police initially believed the incident was an isolated domestic dispute and did not immediately lock down the campus.

Approximately two hours later, at around 9:40 a.m., Cho entered Norris Hall, a classroom building, chained its main entrance doors shut, and systematically moved from room to room shooting students and faculty. Armed with a 9mm Glock 19 and a .22-caliber Walther P22 semi-automatic pistol and carrying hundreds of rounds of ammunition, he killed 30 more people in roughly ten minutes before fatally shooting himself as police breached the building. In total, 32 people were killed — 27 students and 5 faculty members — and at least 17 others were wounded by gunfire, with additional injuries sustained by students who jumped from windows to escape.

Among the faculty victims was Liviu Librescu, a 76-year-old Romanian-born Holocaust survivor and aerospace engineering professor, who used his body to barricade his classroom door and allow students to escape through windows before he was shot and killed. Librescu's death was particularly noted because April 16, 2007, coincided with Yom HaShoah — Holocaust Remembrance Day — intensifying the symbolism of his sacrifice for students in his engineering class.

Cho had a documented history of severe depression and selective mutism diagnosed in eighth grade, and his disturbing creative writings had previously alarmed professors. However, Virginia Tech was unaware of relevant mental health adjudications due to gaps in federal privacy law reporting requirements — gaps that the attack's aftermath moved Congress and the state of Virginia to address.

## Perpetrator Background

Cho Seung-Hui was born on January 18, 1984, in Seoul, South Korea, and immigrated to the United States with his family in 1992, settling in Centreville, Virginia. He received U.S. permanent resident status (a "green card") shortly after. Diagnosed with severe anxiety disorder and selective mutism in middle school, Cho received speech therapy and psychiatric counseling through his school district until he entered Virginia Tech in 2003.

At Virginia Tech, English professors Lucinda Roy and Nikki Giovanni expressed concern about Cho's creative writing — which included plays featuring adolescent rage and murder — and Roy tutored him individually after he was removed from an English class for making female students uncomfortable. Roy attempted to alert university officials about her concerns in 2005; university staff told her that they were unable to intervene unless Cho made a specific threat. In December 2005, a Virginia special justice's court ordered Cho to seek outpatient psychiatric treatment after a Virginia Tech counseling center staff member assessed him as a risk to himself; the court did not find him to be a danger to others, and the adjudication was not reported to the National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS) due to an interpretation of Virginia law that exempted outpatient commitments.

Cho legally purchased both firearms used in the attack — the Walther P22 on February 9, 2007, from an online dealer (TGSCOM Inc.), and the Glock 19 on March 13, 2007, from Roanoke Firearms in Virginia — from licensed firearms dealers. Both purchases required background checks under federal law; because his mental health adjudication had never been reported to NICS, the checks returned no disqualifying information. He purchased 19 magazines (10-round clips) and approximately 400 rounds of ammunition in preparation.

Between the two attacks on April 16, Cho mailed a package to NBC News in New York containing photographs of himself posing with firearms, a 23-page written manifesto articulating grievances against wealthy students and Christianity, and 27 video clips recorded at his dormitory. The package arrived at NBC on April 18 and was aired in edited form on NBC Nightly News on April 18 before the full materials were turned over to FBI investigators; the airing generated controversy about whether media organizations should broadcast materials from mass shooters.

## The Emergency Notification Controversy

A central controversy in the aftermath of the attack was the university's decision not to lock down the campus after the dormitory shooting at 7:15 a.m. — a decision made by the Virginia Tech Policy Group, which met at approximately 7:57 a.m. The group concluded, based on initial police assessments, that the dormitory shooting was likely a domestic incident and that the suspect had fled campus. The first campus-wide email warning was not sent until 9:26 a.m. — more than two hours after the first shooting and 14 minutes before Cho began shooting in Norris Hall. Had the alert been issued earlier, it is widely argued that Norris Hall students and faculty might have had time to leave the building or barricade classrooms.

In March 2011, the U.S. Department of Education fined Virginia Tech $55,000 — the maximum allowed for two violations of the Clery Act (the federal law requiring timely notification of campus threats) — for failing to issue a timely campus warning after the dormitory shooting. The fine was later reduced to $32,500 on appeal, a sum the university paid in 2014.

## Significance

The Virginia Tech shooting was the deadliest mass shooting in modern U.S. history at the time and remains the deadliest school shooting in American history. Its aftermath produced the most significant federal gun-control legislation in over a decade: the NICS Improvement Amendments Act of 2007 (H.R. 2640), sponsored by Representatives Carolyn McCarthy (D-NY) and Jim Ramstad (R-MN), signed by President George W. Bush on January 5, 2008. The law required states to report mental health adjudications to the NICS federal database and provided federal grants to help states upgrade their reporting systems. Within five years of passage, state reporting of mental health records to NICS increased from approximately 800,000 records to more than 5 million.

### Campus Safety Reform

At the state level, Virginia Governor Tim Kaine issued an executive order closing reporting gaps between state and federal systems, and Virginia Tech was subsequently fined by the federal government for failing to issue a timely campus-wide warning after the initial dormitory shooting. Universities nationwide re-examined campus security protocols, emergency notification systems, and mental health services in the attack's wake. The Clery Act, which required colleges to report campus crime statistics, was amended in 2008 to add specific requirements for emergency notification timelines and testing protocols.

### The Massengill Report

The Virginia Tech Review Panel, chaired by retired Colonel Gerald Massengill, published its findings in August 2007 as the "Massengill Report." The 147-page report detailed systemic failures across law enforcement, university administration, mental health systems, and privacy law; it made 70 specific recommendations covering emergency communication, mental health privacy law reform, gun purchase records reporting, and academic due-process procedures. The report's recommendations shaped subsequent federal and state legislation and were widely adopted by universities as a self-assessment framework.

### International Dimension

South Korean President Roh Moo-hyun expressed formal condolences and acknowledged the "shock and sadness" of the Korean-American community; the South Korean ambassador in Washington publicly expressed concern about potential backlash against Korean and Korean-American communities in the United States. In the event, no significant backlash occurred; statements by victims' families and university officials emphasized that responsibility lay solely with Cho individually. The event drew condolences from world leaders including Pope Benedict XVI and Queen Elizabeth II.
